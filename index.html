Businessman Johann Rupert helps to award property rights in South Africa
Dec 13, 2019 | By Billie Cannon

South African billionaire Johann Rupert and his wife Gaynor, worked extremely hard with the local authorities of Stellenbosch and the Free Market Foundation to highlight the legal status of bad property ownership. Hundreds have been handed over to the Land Registry, more to come.

In South Africa, the awarding of property rights to people in poverty is particularly challenging because the homes in which they live are stuck in complex land registry systems. 

The pair gave Kaya Mandi, Klapmuts, Kylemore, Franschhoek, and Le Roux 132 full title deeds. This was achieved through Rupert’s goodwill and the actions of Mayor Cllr Gesie van Deventer of Stellenbosch and her team working with the Free Market Foundation (FMF) Khaya Lam (my home) on land reform in Stellenb’s historic town hall.

The ceremony was also witnessed by the Master of Ceremonies Tabiso Mfeya (Head of Planning and Economic Development at Stellenbosch), Leon Louw (Executive Director of FMF), and Temba Nolutshungu (President of Cape Town at FMF).

For more than 40 years, the FMF has advocated the cause of transforming the various types of colonial titles found in townships into the complete, unmistakable property of current owners. Ruperts currently financed 1,000 titles in Stellenbosch and 1,000 in Graff Reinet, where Dr. Rupert’s father Anton was born. To date, 585 title documents have been issued in Stellenbosch, more will be forthcoming.

In cooperation with Khaya Lam and the Municipality, Johann and Gaynor Rupert have made a significant difference in the lives of ordinary South Africans and, in specific, the poorest people living in Stellenbosch. The common goal is to bring about a sincere social and economic transformation through ownership of property. Once the occupants have entered the building, they enter the hall as tenants. Once they leave, they become homeowners in possession of a completely tradable freeholder contract, taking their first step towards a more real economic empowerment.

In his speech before the ceremony of the title deed, Johann Rupert claimed that his family was incredibly fortunate in the wealth they had accumulated and needed to make use of it for the greater good.

“It is easy to give away money but more difficult to find organizations that spend the time to use the funds properly and efficiently,” he said and commended the FMF and Khaya Lam for their ability to do this.
